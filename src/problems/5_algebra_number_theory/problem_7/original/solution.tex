Suppose that there is an equilateral triangle with rational vertices
$\paren{x_1, y_1}, \paren{x_2, y_2}, \paren{x_3, y_3}$.
Its area is
$\mfrac{1}{2} \abs{x_1 y_2 - x_1 y_3 \, + \, x_2 y_3 - x_2 y_1 \, + \, x_3 y_1 - x_3 y_2}$,
which is rational.
And its side length $l$ is such that
$l^2 = \paren{x_2 - x_1}^2 + \paren{y_2 - y_1}^2$,
so $l^2$ is rational. \newline
The area of the triangle is also $\mfrac{1}{4} l^2 \sqrt{3}$,
which is irrational (because $l^2$ is rational and $\sqrt{3}$ is irrational).
This is a contradiction, thus proving that an equilateral triangle cannot have all vertices on rational points.
