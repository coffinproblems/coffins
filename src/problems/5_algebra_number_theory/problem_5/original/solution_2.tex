If $\sqrt{2}^{\sqrt{2}}$ is rational,
then $a = \sqrt{2}$ and $b = \sqrt{2}$ are irrational numbers
such that $a^b$ is rational;
otherwise, $\sqrt{2}^{\sqrt{2}}$ is irrational,
so $a = \sqrt{2}^{\sqrt{2}}$ and $b = \sqrt{2}$ are irrational numbers
such that $a^b = 2$ is rational.

For any $a \in \mathbb{R}^{+} \smallsetminus \braces{1}$, the function
$r: \mathbb{R} \smallsetminus \mathbb{Q} \to \mathbb{R} : x \mapsto a^x$
is a bijection on its image, hence
$\card\paren{\img\paren{r}}
 = \card\paren{\mathbb{R} \smallsetminus \mathbb{Q}}
 = \card\paren{\mathbb{R}}
 > \card\paren{\mathbb{Q}}$;
in particular $\img\paren{r} \nsubseteq \mathbb{Q}$.
So, for every irrational $a > 0$,
there exist a continuum of irrational numbers $b > 0$
such that $a^b \in \mathbb{R} \smallsetminus \mathbb{Q}$.
